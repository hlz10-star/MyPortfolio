Timber Chair
Design Challenge

About the Project

In this design challenge, my team was tasked with designing and building a timber chair using provided materials. The chair had to withstand progressively higher weight loads while staying within a strict budget. The goal was to balance strength, cost, and structural efficiency.

About the Chair

Our chair was inspired by a modern dining chair. Our strategy was to use as little wood as possible while maximizing the weight it could hold. To increase stability, we added four beams to the chair legs for extra support. We also replaced the planned cross-beam backrest with a square support frame beneath the seat plate, which significantly improved load distribution. We painted the chair brown and added fabric and thread as decorative elements.

Budget & Materials

Results

Our chair successfully held 140 lbs — including Dr. Sanchez — without failure.

Cost-to-Weight Ratio
491 ÷ 140 = 3.51 ₣ per lb of weight supported
Factor of Safety
140 lbs ÷ 200 lbs = 0.7 — below 1.0, meaning the chair would not meet a 200 lb safety standard, but it exceeded our team's expectations.
What Worked
Adding a square support frame beneath the seat plate was the key decision that improved stability and load distribution. Extra screws and reinforcement at the joints also reduced wobble despite imperfect leg alignment.
Unexpected Results
The chair performed better than anticipated. We initially worried the legs weren't perfectly aligned, but the tight fastening and support frame compensated. The wood itself was also stronger than we assumed.
Real-World Applications
A composite beam combines materials like steel, concrete, and wood so each contributes its strongest properties. This improves strength-to-weight ratio and reduces material use — the same principles we applied to our chair by combining structural elements to distribute loads more efficiently.

Robotic Rube Goldberg
Design Challenge

About the Project

In this design challenge, my team was tasked with designing and constructing a Rube Goldberg machine to complete a simple task. Our machine was designed to make a bowl of cereal from start to finish — dispensing a spoon, pouring cereal, and adding milk — all triggered by a single human impulse. The machine included simple mechanisms, complex mechanisms, and a mechatronic mechanism incorporating an Arduino microcontroller.

Mechanism 1 — Spoon Drop

A rope and pulley system was used to spin an arm that pushed a spoon off a ramp and into the bowl. A lower arm simultaneously hit a ball into a domino chain to trigger the following steps. A custom 3D-printed ball bearing was used to reduce friction in the spinning motion.

Mechanism 2 — Cereal Dispenser

A colorful domino chain reaction knocked over a lightweight cup containing a small amount of cereal. The dominos transferred kinetic energy through rotational motion, tipping the cup forward so cereal fell into a funnel and into the moving cart's bowl.

Mechanism 3 — Lever Arm Milk Dispenser

This was both the complex and mechatronic mechanism. A photoresistor detected when dark liquid was poured into a beaker above it, triggering an Arduino to rotate a servo motor 90°, pouring milk from a cup into the cereal bowl below. A 10kΩ resistor was used to prevent false positives.

Mechanism 4 — Motorized Cereal Cart

A motorized cart with a fishing line attached to a DC motor pulled the cereal bowl forward to catch ingredients at the right time. Activated by a pressure sensor, the motor was coded to stop and start at intervals to sync with the other mechanisms.

Results

Our machine did not successfully complete all mechanisms in one run during the in-class demo due to issues with the domino chain. However, every individual mechanism worked correctly on its own. A video of a successful attempt was recorded separately.

What Worked
Each individual mechanism functioned correctly when tested separately. The motorized cart, spoon drop, and milk dispenser all performed as designed. The 3D-printed bearing significantly reduced friction in the spoon drop mechanism.
Challenges Faced
The domino chain was the weakest link — inconsistent spacing and lightweight dominos couldn't reliably knock over the cereal cup. Losing a team member mid-project also required us to redistribute work and pivot several designs under time pressure.
My Role
I designed and built the cereal dropping mechanism using a domino chain reaction. I was responsible for optimizing the domino spacing, cup angle, and cereal amount to ensure consistency. I also helped test the motors and light sensor.
Key Takeaways
I deepened my understanding of how force, torque, and mass affect motion. I improved my prototyping and iterative design skills through repeated testing of the domino chain, and strengthened my ability to collaborate under time constraints.

Designing Flavors &
Scaling Parties

About the Project

In this design challenge, my team applied industrial engineering principles to the challenge of large-scale meal preparation. We designed an Excel-based tool to help chefs scale recipes and manage the logistics of serving anywhere from 50 to 2,000 guests — covering ingredient scaling, production scheduling, labor allocation, and temperature control. We also cooked a stir fry in studio and used it as the basis for our model.

Excel Tool Components

1 · Recipe Scaling Sheet
A formula-based tool that automatically adjusts ingredient quantities based on guest count. The original recipe serves 10, and a scaling factor is applied: Scaled Quantity = Original Quantity × (Guests ÷ 10). I was responsible for this component.
2 · Production Schedule
A Gantt chart with 2-minute task increments covering prep, cooking, plating, and serving. Work was evenly distributed among team members, with 8 minutes of unallocated buffer time each for corrections or helping teammates.
3 · Labor Allocation
A workforce planner calculating the minimum staff needed for different tasks. As guest count increases, staff increases at a decreasing rate to avoid overcrowding — except servers and transporters, which scale proportionally.
4 · Temperature Control
A manual temperature logging system with a visualization graph and indicator showing whether required food temperatures have been reached, using VLOOKUP to match food types to their required temperatures.

Results & Reflection

Our team successfully built a fully functional Excel model covering all four required components. The stir fry recipe was scaled using our tool, and the production schedule kept our team synchronized throughout the cooking session. Key bottlenecks — such as pan heating time and chicken broth preparation — were identified and addressed through strategic task reordering.

Foam Core Enclosure
Design Challenge

About the Project

In this design challenge, I created a foam core enclosure with a maximum volume of 1 gallon using foam core prototyping techniques. I then exchanged my enclosure with a partner, who added a hidden compartment to mine — and I added a hidden compartment to theirs. I designed a Pokéball as my enclosure.

My Enclosure — The Pokéball

I designed and built a Pokéball-shaped foam core enclosure. The left photo shows the enclosure before painting, and the right shows the final painted version.

Hidden Compartment — Partner's Enclosure

I received my partner's enclosure and added a hidden compartment inside. The left photo shows the full enclosure from the outside, and the right reveals the hidden compartment within.

Design Process
I chose to design a Pokéball because of its recognizable spherical shape, which made it a fun challenge to construct using flat foam core sheets. I had to carefully plan the geometry to achieve the rounded form.
Key Takeaways
This challenge strengthened my foam core prototyping skills and taught me how to think about enclosure design — balancing aesthetics, structural integrity, and hidden functionality within a volume constraint.
